Overview: The Static Potential Well

A Static Potential Well is a region of space in which the electrostatic potential energy reaches a local minimum (or maximum), effectively "trapping" or "ruling" the charge state of everything within its reach. Unlike a simple point charge or a uniform electric field, a potential well creates a spatial gradient that governs how nearby charges—and the matter those charges belong to—behave.

⚡

This site examines the intersection of high-voltage electrostatics, alloy material science, and plasma formation to explore whether a craft surrounded by such a field could achieve propulsion, shielding, and environmental dominance simultaneously.

The foundational question: Can a carefully engineered metallic alloy maintain a permanent, high-gradient electrostatic charge that warps its local environment into a self-sustaining plasma boundary—without continuous external power input?

The Biefeld-Brown Effect

In the late 1920s, physicist Paul Alfred Biefeld and engineer Thomas Townsend Brown independently observed that a capacitor with asymmetric electrodes, when charged to a high voltage, produced a net mechanical force directed from the larger (negative) electrode toward the smaller (positive) electrode. This phenomenon became known as the Biefeld-Brown Effect.

Documented Observations

  • A charged capacitor suspended on a balance moves toward its positive terminal.
  • The effect scales with voltage (Brown reported useful thrust in the 50–100 kV range).
  • The effect persists in vacuum, though at reduced magnitude, indicating both ionic-wind and a non-ionic component.
  • Brown's "electrokinetic apparatus" patents (US 2,949,550 and US 3,187,206) describe disc-shaped craft that convert electrostatic energy directly into thrust.

Proposed Mechanisms

  • Ionic Wind (EHD): Electrohydrodynamic drag on air molecules ionised near the sharp electrode creates a fluid thrust.
  • Electrostatic Pressure: A charge asymmetry produces a net radiation-pressure-like impulse on the capacitor body itself.
  • Asymmetric Capacitance Gradient: The electric field lines concentrate on one face, producing an imbalanced Maxwell stress tensor.

Maxwell Stress Tensor (simplified)

The electrostatic pressure on a surface element is:

P = ε₀/2 · E²

where ε₀ is the permittivity of free space (8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m) and E is the local electric field magnitude (V/m). An asymmetric body with Efront ≫ Eback experiences a net force.

Work Function & the Electrostatic Alloy

The work function (Φ) of a material is the minimum energy required to remove an electron from its surface into a vacuum. A high work function material holds onto its electrons tightly—meaning it can sustain a strong surface charge without spontaneously emitting electrons that would neutralise it.

By engineering an alloy whose crystal structure presents high-work-function faces on all exposed surfaces—and whose bulk resistivity is low enough to distribute the charge uniformly—it is theoretically possible to create a permanent electrostatic battery: a body that holds a fixed surface charge indefinitely without an external power source, provided the environment does not supply a discharge path.

Faraday Shielding & Field Dominance

A Faraday cage is a conductive enclosure that redistributes external electric fields around its surface, cancelling them inside. A sufficiently charged conductive hull takes this further: instead of passively cancelling external fields, it actively imposes its own field on the surrounding medium.

Passive Shielding

Standard Faraday cage: external fields induce surface charges that cancel the internal field. The cage is electrically neutral overall. Any incoming electromagnetic wave is attenuated by the skin depth of the conductor.

Active / Dominant Field

A hull carrying a net charge Q creates an external field E = kQ/r². At sufficient charge density, this field ionises the surrounding air, creating a plasma layer. The plasma further reflects incoming EM radiation (plasma frequency cut-off), producing a compound shield: electrostatic + plasma.

Plasma Frequency Cut-off

ω_p = √(n·e² / ε₀·m_e)

EM waves with frequency below ωp cannot propagate through the plasma layer and are reflected. A dense plasma (n ≈ 10¹⁸ m⁻³) cuts off frequencies up to several GHz—covering most radar and communications bands.

Plasma Boundary — The Vacuum Seal

When the electric field at the hull surface exceeds the dielectric breakdown strength of air (~3 MV/m at sea level), the surrounding air ionises into a plasma. This plasma sheath has several remarkable properties:

  • Aerodynamic drag reduction: The plasma alters the boundary layer, reducing viscous drag significantly (studies by NASA and USAF have demonstrated drag reduction of 20–40% with plasma actuators).
  • Radar absorption/reflection: The plasma layer reflects or absorbs radar frequencies below the plasma frequency, providing stealth characteristics.
  • Thermal protection: The ionised sheath stands off re-entry heat in hypersonic vehicles (re-entry plasma is a naturally occurring version of this effect).
  • Acoustic isolation: Density discontinuity at the plasma boundary attenuates pressure waves, reducing sonic signature.
  • Self-sealing: If the hull charge is maintained, the plasma layer self-repairs around any disturbance, as additional air molecules are immediately ionised to fill the gap.
🌡️

The critical engineering challenge is sustaining the surface charge density required to maintain breakdown (σ > ε₀ · 3×10⁶ V/m ≈ 26.5 μC/m²) continuously across the entire hull, including during manoeuvres that compress or rarefy the plasma on different faces.
